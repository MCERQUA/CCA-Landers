Frequently Asked Questions About Glazing Contractor Insurance

What types of insurance do glazing contractors need?

Glazing contractors typically need several types of insurance including: - General Liability Insurance (covers glass breakage and installation errors) - Workers' Compensation Insurance - Commercial Auto Insurance (for transporting glass) - Inland Marine Insurance (for glass lifts and cutting equipment) - Professional Liability Insurance (for design/engineering work) - Umbrella/Excess Liability Insurance

Each provides different coverage for the unique risks of glass installation work.

How much does glazing contractor insurance cost?

Glazing contractor insurance costs vary based on: - Annual revenue and project types (commercial vs residential) - Number of employees and their experience levels - Geographic location and local regulations - Claims history and safety record - Coverage limits and deductibles

Typical annual premiums range from $3,000-$7,000 for small glazing businesses, with larger operations paying $10,000+ depending on risk factors.

Do glazing contractors need workers' comp for themselves?

For glazing contractors working solo:

  • Most states don't require sole proprietors to carry workers' comp for themselves
  • However, glass installation work has unique risks (cuts, falls, lifting injuries)
  • Many GCs and building owners require proof of coverage before hiring glaziers
  • Some states mandate coverage for all construction trades

We recommend coverage even if not required - glass work injuries can be costly without insurance.

What does general liability cover for glazing contractors?

For glazing contractors, general liability insurance covers:

  • Glass breakage during installation or transport
  • Damage to client property from installation work
  • Injuries to third parties from glass shards or tools
  • Advertising claims (copyright, slander, etc.)
  • Completed operations (issues after project completion)

It does NOT cover: - Damage to your own equipment/tools - Employee injuries (requires workers' comp) - Professional design errors (requires E&O insurance)

Why is glazing insurance more expensive than some other trades?

Glazing insurance costs more due to unique risks:

  1. Glass is fragile and expensive to replace when broken
  2. High-rise installations carry fall risks and liability
  3. Specialized equipment (lifts, cutters) increases injury potential
  4. Glass defects can cause delayed failure claims
  5. Installation errors often require complete rework
  6. Thermal stress and structural movement risks

Insurers price based on these glazing-specific risk factors.
